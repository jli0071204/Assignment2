U.S. MILITARISM &
VIETNAMESE REFUGEES

How U.S. Military Intervention in Vietnam Manufactured a Refugee Crisis and Reinforced Global Inequality
Thesis

Looking at nine data visualizations tracing the Vietnamese diaspora from wartime destruction to present-day outcomes in the United States, I argue that U.S. militarism in Vietnam, through aerial bombardment, chemical warfare, and a 19-year postwar trade embargo, produced mass forced migration and funneled displaced populations through U.S. military installations into a receiving society where measurable gaps in poverty, income, and education persisted for decades, reproducing class inequality long after the war ended.

More Bombs Than All of WWII

From 1965 to 1968, Operation Rolling Thunder dropped over 800,000 tons of ordnance on North Vietnam alone, while secret B-52 carpet bombing campaigns leveled large areas of Cambodia and Laos. The combined 7.66 million tons, 3.6 times the total used in all of World War II, destroyed rice paddies, irrigation canals, and entire villages, eliminating the agricultural base that had sustained rural Vietnamese communities for generations and leaving millions with no land to farm and no livelihood to return to.

Refugee Arrivals and Immigration Pathways

The first wave left during the chaotic April 1975 evacuation, when U.S. helicopters airlifted evacuees off rooftops in Saigon while thousands fought to board departing Navy ships at the port. The larger wave came during the boat people crisis of the late 1970s, when hundreds of thousands crowded onto fishing boats and risked drowning in the South China Sea to reach camps in Thailand, Malaysia, and the Philippines. Arrivals continued into the early 1990s through the Orderly Departure Program, a channel the U.S. and Vietnamese governments set up precisely because people were still desperate to leave over a decade after the war ended.

From 231,000 to 1.33 Million

The Refugee Act of 1980 allowed resettled refugees to sponsor relatives still in Vietnam, turning an initial population of 231,000 into over 1.3 million by 2022. Greater concentrations developed near Camp Pendleton in California, Fort Chaffee in Arkansas, and along the Gulf Coast in Texas and Louisiana, where one displaced officer or translator who arrived in 1975 could by the 1990s bring over parents, siblings, and their children, multiplying one act of displacement across three generations.

Rejected by the Public

A May 1975 Gallup poll found that 54% of Americans opposed admitting Vietnamese refugees. Governors in California and Arkansas publicly asked the federal government not to resettle refugees in their states, and labor unions argued that incoming Vietnamese would take American jobs during the 1975 recession. The hostility went hand-in-hand with broader post-Vietnam disillusionment, leaving refugees with no English, no savings, and no connections to build a life in a country that did not want them.

Poverty After Arrival

In 1980, 35.8% of Vietnamese Americans lived below the poverty line. Many who had been teachers, military officers, or civil servants in Vietnam ended up in nail salons, assembly lines, and shrimp boats along the Gulf Coast, taking whatever work required no English. The rate fell over two decades but leveled off around 15-17% after 2000 and stayed there through 2015, well after the initial adjustment period had passed.

The Embargo's 242-to-1 Impact

From 1975 to 1994, the U.S. maintained a trade embargo that blocked Vietnam from international markets, World Bank loans, and IMF support, pushing GDP per capita to $99 by 1990 while the U.S. stood at $23,889. Vietnam launched Doi Moi economic reforms in 1986, but the embargo limited their effect until President Clinton lifted it in 1994, nearly two decades after the war ended.

Income Gap Within "Asian America"

Vietnamese households earn $86,000 annually, above the U.S. median of $75,500 but $19,600 below the Asian American median of $105,600. Many Indian and Chinese Americans entered through H-1B work visas with graduate degrees and tech salaries already in hand, while Vietnamese families arrived through refugee camps with no credentials and no capital. Averaging these groups under one "Asian American" label makes the refugee-origin gap invisible.

Educational Disadvantage Built into Arrival

Vietnamese immigrants are four times more likely than U.S.-born Americans to lack a high school diploma. Schools across South Vietnam were destroyed or shut down during the war, and many boat people were young adults from rural provinces who left before finishing their education. The lack of schooling funneled them into lower-paying manual work upon arrival, feeding into the poverty and income gaps shown above.

Conclusion

The 7.66 million tons of ordnance dropped across Indochina (Fig. 1) destroyed the land Vietnamese communities depended on, forcing an exodus routed through U.S. military bases at Clark AFB, Guam, and Camp Pendleton (Fig. 2). Boat people fled through the 1980s and the Orderly Departure Program extended arrivals into the 1990s (Fig. 3), while family reunification grew the displaced population sixfold (Fig. 4). Upon arrival, 54% of Americans wanted refugees kept out (Fig. 5), and former professionals ended up in nail salons and shrimp boats at a 35.8% poverty rate that never fell below 15% (Fig. 6). The 19-year trade embargo blocked Vietnam from rebuilding, widening the GDP gap to 242-to-1 (Fig. 7), while Vietnamese households today still earn $19,600 below the Asian American median (Fig. 8) and are four times more likely to lack a high school diploma than the U.S.-born population (Fig. 9). As a result, and according to the data above, Vietnamese refugees have sought to rebuild lives shaped by conditions U.S. militarism itself created, from the bombing that destroyed their communities to the embargo that prevented return to the reception that limited their advancement for decades afterward.
